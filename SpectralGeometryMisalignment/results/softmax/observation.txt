THEORETICAL SETUP
-----------------
X = raw node feature matrix of the LCC (no diffusion applied)
Y = restricted eigenvectors of X via Rayleigh-Ritz:
    solve (Q^T L Q)V = (Q^T D Q)V Lambda
    where Q = orthonormal basis of span(X), L = D - A, D = degree matrix
    Result: Y^T D Y = I (D-orthonormal), span(Y) = span(X) exactly

Theoretical guarantee: softmax regression (single linear layer, cross-entropy,
zero weight decay) is convex. Since span(X) = span(Y), the global optimum
accuracy must be identical for both representations. Differences in final
accuracy measure optimization failure, not information loss.

Why the guarantee holds: since span(X) = span(Y), any linear classifier
on X can be exactly rewritten as a linear classifier on Y by reparameterizing
the weight matrix through the change of basis, and vice versa. The two problems
have the same set of achievable decision boundaries. Therefore their global
minima — their best possible linear accuracy — must be identical. Any gap
that remains after 500 epochs is a statement about how Y's geometry makes
that minimum harder to reach, not about whether the minimum exists.

UNDERDETERMINATION RATIO — INTERPRETATION
------------------------------------------
r = (d_eff * C) / n_train = parameters / training examples

r << 1: overdetermined. Solution is unique. GD finds it reliably for
        both X and Y. Theoretical guarantee holds in practice.
r ~= 1: near-balanced. Small gaps possible.
r >> 1: underdetermined. Infinitely many solutions fit training data
        perfectly. Which one GD finds depends on the geometry of the
        representation. X and Y have different geometry after
        D-orthonormalization, so they attract GD to different solutions
        with different generalization. The guarantee still holds in
        theory (same global minimum exists) but GD does not reach it
        in 500 epochs — and reaches different near-minima for X vs Y.

This is not a failure of the experiment. It is the finding.
The convex setting removes the "local minima" objection and isolates
exactly the geometric effect of D-orthonormalization on optimization.

================================================================================
TWO DISTINCT FAILURE MECHANISMS
================================================================================

MECHANISM 1 — X sparsity advantage (Cora, CiteSeer fixed splits):
  Applies when: ratio >> 1 AND X has many zero columns on training nodes
  What happens: X's effective rank << d due to sparse features.
  GD finds generalizable direction because fewer free parameters.
  Y has dense equal-variance columns → more free parameters → memorizes.
  Confound: gap here is partly about X's sparsity, not only Y's geometry.

MECHANISM 2 — Y's optimization geometry (Coauthor-CS, Coauthor-Physics):
  Applies when: ratio > 1 AND both X and Y are fully dense
  What happens: D-orthonormalization equalizes variance across all Y columns.
  Y's loss landscape is harder to navigate than X's regardless of dimensionality.
  SGD: slow convergence, ~5-6x more epochs needed (mean 5.6x, range 5.0-6.0x
       across 15 seeds), gap ~18 pp at epoch 500.
  Adam: memorization collapse, loss crashes while early generalization stalls
        (Y val rises from 16% → 22% in first 10 epochs, then freezes).
  This is the purest measurement of the D-orthonormalization geometry effect.

CLEANEST RESULT FOR THE PAPER:
  Coauthor-CS / SGD / random splits / lr=0.01:
  - Both X and Y fully dense, 10,999 training nodes
  - Same effective dimensionality, no sparsity confound
  - Gap = +18 pp at epoch 500, Y loss still decreasing
  - Purely geometric: D-orthonormalization makes Y harder to optimize
  - Convex model, no local minima — gap cannot be dismissed as
    "optimizer got stuck in wrong local minimum"
